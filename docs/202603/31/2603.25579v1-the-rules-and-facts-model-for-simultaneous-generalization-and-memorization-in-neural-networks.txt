# The Rules-and-Facts Model for Simultaneous Generalization and Memorization in Neural Networks 

Gabriele Farné 1, Fabrizio Boncoraglio 1, Lenka Zdeborová 1 

> 1

Statistical Physics of Computation Laboratory, École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL) CH-1015 Lausanne 

Abstract 

A key capability of modern neural networks is their capacity to simultaneously learn underlying rules and memorize specific facts or exceptions. Yet, theoretical understanding of this dual capability remains limited. We introduce the Rules-and-Facts (RAF) model, a minimal solvable setting that enables precise characterization of this phenomenon by bridging two classical lines of work in the statistical physics of learning: the teacher–student framework for generalization and Gardner-style capacity analysis for memorization. In the RAF model, a fraction 1 − ε of training labels is generated by a structured teacher rule, while a fraction 

ε consists of unstructured facts with random labels. We characterize when the learner can simultaneously recover the underlying rule—allowing generalization to new data—and memorize the unstructured examples. Our results quantify how overparameterization enables the simultaneous realization of these two objectives: sufficient excess capacity supports memorization, while regularization and the choice of kernel or nonlinearity control the allocation of capacity between rule learning and memorization. The RAF model provides a theoretical foundation for understanding how modern neural networks can infer structure while storing rare or non-compressible information. 

# 1 Introduction 

A hallmark of modern neural networks is their ability to simultaneously infer structured patterns and store specific exceptions or facts [ 1 –3 ]. For example, it is desirable to learn a rule for the addition of two numbers rather than storing in the network’s weights the full list of possible sums. At the same time, certain facts—such as the name of the capital of France—likely need to be memorized. A language model, for instance, can generalize grammatical rules such as English verb conjugation (“jump” becomes “jumped”) while also memorizing irregular forms (“go” becomes “went”). It is perhaps surprising that, unlike biological systems in which reasoning and memory are often attributed to distinct mechanisms or specialized brain regions, see e.g. [ 4], modern neural networks—such as transformers—perform both functions within a single architecture. Understanding how and when modern neural networks are capable of generalizing and memorizing simultaneously remains a foundational open question in current machine learning, and is the main motivation for the present work. Modern AI systems also exhibit regimes where they produce plausible but incorrect facts; see Appendix E for an illustrative example, including a hallucinated reference to a non-existent paper by Mei, Montanari, and Nguyen. Understanding how generalization can coexist with reliable memorization is, therefore, not only of theoretical interest but also of practical relevance. 

Memorization vs Generalization: A Long-Standing Tension In classical learning theory, generalization and memorization have typically been viewed as competing or even mutually exclusive phenomena – contributing to the long-held view that “generalization begins where memorization ends”. The classical Rademacher - complexity bounds formalize this view by showing 1

> arXiv:2603.25579v1 [stat.ML] 26 Mar 2026

that models with insufficient capacity to memorize arbitrary labelings are precisely those that are guaranteed to generalize [5, 6]. A drastic shift in understanding came with recent efforts to explain why modern neural networks can generalize even in highly overparameterized regimes—despite also being able to fit purely random or partially randomized labels [ 7 ]. In that work, memorization was primarily used as a stress test for model capacity rather than as a desirable learning objective. An influential line of work has since demonstrated that memorization and generalization are not inherently at odds: overfitting associated with memorization can, in fact, be benign and need not impair a model’s ability to generalize [ 8 ]. This insight is closely linked to the now broadly studied double-descent phenomenon, in which test error initially worsens and then improves again as the number of model parameters increases beyond the so-called interpolation threshold [ 9 –11 ]. Certain works explore this interplay even further by showing that memorization of a small subset of rare or atypical examples can, in fact, enhance generalization performance [12, 13]. Despite this shift in perspective, much of the recent literature still treats memorization as something to be tolerated – benign at best, but not intrinsically desirable. In these works, the emphasis remains on understanding how generalization can persist despite the model’s capacity to memorize, rather than on settings where memorization is necessary for successful performance. Separately, memorization has been studied through models such as the perceptron with random labels [ 14 ], or in associative memory networks like the Hopfield model [ 15 , 16 ], which explicitly store patterns but do not generalize beyond them. While these classical setups have been generalized to the context of modern architectures [ 17 –21 ], they further reinforced the notion of separation between generalization and memorization. Other lines of work treat memorization and generalization as fundamentally antagonistic. In privacy-sensitive applications such as medical diagnostics or text generation, memorization of specific training examples is considered a failure mode—raising concerns about data leakage and the unintended exposure of sensitive information. This has motivated extensive research on auditing, avoiding, or reversing memorization in trained models, particularly in the context of differential privacy and machine unlearning [ 22 – 24 ]. A perspective analogous to memorization being undesirable arises in generative modeling, where memorizing the training set leads to overfitting and degraded sample quality. For instance, diffusion models are expected to learn the underlying data distribution rather than reproduce memorized training samples, and recent works show that indeed diffusion models start to generalize once they fail to memorize [ 25 –27 ]. These works further reinforce the conventional view that memorization compromises generalization and should be suppressed or mitigated. 

Memorization and Generalization Must Go Hand in Hand In contrast to lines of work that treat memorization as an undesirable—or at best tolerable—side effect, our work is motivated by the observation that, in many realistic tasks, generalization and memorization are not merely compatible, but jointly essential. This is nowhere more evident than in the performance of modern large language models (LLMs), which routinely demonstrate the ability to infer grammatical and semantic structure while also recalling specific factual content. These models simultaneously solve tasks requiring abstract reasoning (e.g., code synthesis, translation, summarization) and factual precision (e.g., recalling scientific knowledge, historical dates, or rare terminology). Rather than viewing memorization as a nuisance or limitation, we treat it as an integral component of the learning objective, on equal footing with generalization. Despite the centrality of this phenomenon in modern AI, there is—remarkably—no theoretical framework to date that captures generalization and memorization simultaneously and allows their interplay to be analyzed in closed form. 

The Rules-and-Facts (RAF) Task Model Modern AI models must both generalize patterns and memorize facts – any complete theory of learning must account for both. To advance toward 2such a theory, we introduce an analytically tractable framework, which we call the Rules-and-Facts (RAF) model , that enables the study of generalization and memorization as simultaneous and necessary objectives. In the RAF model, each training label is either generated by a structured teacher rule or assigned at random as an isolated fact that must be memorized. A parameter ε ∈ [0 , 1] controls the proportion of factual (non-generalizable) examples, with the remaining 1 − ε fraction adhering to the structured rule. This formulation captures the fundamental tension—and potential synergy—between generalization and memorization within a unified theoretical framework. The analyzed neural networks A central question, then, is under what conditions a learner can succeed at both tasks – learning the underlying rule and memorizing the unstructured facts – and how this ability depends on structural properties such as model architecture or regularization. As suggested by existing results, successful memorization of arbitrary facts requires a model with sufficiently many parameters. The simplest overparameterized models that remain analytically tractable in the high-dimensional regime are the kernel regression and the related random features regression [ 30 ]. By analyzing their performance on the RAF model, we can quantitatively characterize the interplay between generalization (achieved by learning the rule) and memorization of the facts. Evaluation quantities The performance of all the models is then evaluated on two downstream tasks. 

• Factual recall task : When the learned model sees back a training sample for which the label was random, it should return back that label. By desiring good performance on this task, we model the ability to recall facts. 

• Teacher-rule task : The model should be able to apply the teacher rule on previously unseen input samples. By desiring this, we model the ability to apply the learned rule in new contexts. We are seeking a model that is able to perform well on both the factual recall and teacher-rule tasks, equivalent to memorizing and generalizing at the same time. This also clarifies that the fact-labels cannot be treated merely as noise in a standard teacher–student setting. If they were simply noise, the objective would be to ignore them. In contrast, in the RAF model, performance on the factual recall task is explicitly required. This makes the RAF model conceptually distinct from previously studied settings that do not simultaneously treat both the factual recall and teacher-rule tasks. Key take-away A central mechanism emerging from our analysis is that, in overparameterized models, different components of the representation can be allocated to distinct objectives. In particular, the kernel geometry effectively separates a component aligned with the teacher rule from components that can be used to interpolate unstructured labels. As we will show, this decomposition — captured by the parameters μ1 and μ⋆ (defined in Eq. (30) ) – controls how capacity is distributed between generalization and memorization, and thereby enables their simultaneous realization. This perspective provides a simple interpretation of the role of overparameterization: excess capacity is not merely used to fit arbitrary data, but can be selectively allocated so as to preserve alignment with the underlying rule while storing non-compressible information. In this sense, the coexistence of generalization and memorization is not paradoxical, but a direct consequence of how capacity is organized in overparametrized models.

3.1 The classical trade-off between generalization and memorization

3.2 The power of overparameterization 

More expressive models, i.e., in general those having more trainable parameters, will have a larger capacity to memorize samples. The key question is whether such memorization can be achieved without sacrificing generalization. To start investigating this question, we also plot in Fig. 1 the parametric curve λ 7 → (Egen (λ), Emem (λ)) for two representative kernel regressions, one corresponding to the infinitely overparameterized random features with erf activation (in orange), the other to ReLU activation (in green). The corresponding kernels are given in Eq. (33). 11 The results show that overparameterization dramatically improves the set of simultaneously low errors (Egen , Emem ) achievable by varying the regularization λ. The corresponding curves in Fig. 1 demonstrate that approaching λ → 0+ does not necessarily entail catastrophic loss of generalization: for a wide range of λ, kernel regression can simultaneously attain small Emem and a generalization error that remains low. In other words, overparameterization creates a regime of useful (and in the RAF model, necessary) benign overfitting: the model uses excess capacity to memorize the unstructured fraction of the data while preserving a representation aligned with the teacher rule. This observation is conceptually distinct from much of the benign-overfitting literature [ 8, 32 ], where memorization is typically treated as a by-product to be tolerated. In the RAF model, memorization is part of the task objective: the learner is expected to store the factual subset. In Fig. 1 we see that, in particular, the ReLU kernel enables regimes in which the model fits the random labels nearly perfectly while maintaining good generalization. The erf kernel does not achieve good memorization and generalization simultaneously. This motivates a follow-up question of how the generalization-memorization trade-off depends on the kernel, i.e., the two parameters μ1, μ⋆, and the data, via the ε, α parameters. # 4 Conclusion 

We introduced the Rules-and-Facts (RAF) framework to initiate a theoretical study of how neural networks can simultaneously generalize structured rules and memorize unstructured facts. While both capabilities are central to modern AI systems, they have largely been examined in isolation within existing theoretical studies. Our analysis, carried out in the high-dimensional limit, reveals a clear structural picture. Linear models exhibit an unavoidable trade-off between rule learning and fact memorization. In contrast, overparameterized models — even in their simplest analytically tractable form of random features and kernel regression — admit regimes where memorization and generalization coexist. Overparameterization emerges as the key enabler, providing sufficient excess capacity to store factual exceptions while preserving alignment with the underlying rule. Regularization strength λ and kernel geometry, through a single parameter γ, defined in Eq. (38) , then govern how this capacity is allocated. Beyond the specific results, the RAF framework provides a minimal setting in which tensions commonly observed in practice — benign overfitting, interpolation, and capacity allocation — can be analyzed quantitatively and in closed form. Several open directions naturally follow. First, extending the analysis to feature-learning architectures, where representations them-selves adapt, may clarify how faster generalization rates (e.g., approaching Bayes-optimal scaling) are compatible with simultaneous memorization. Second, richer architectures such as multi-layer networks and attention-based models may allow the study of how different functional components specialize, potentially separating rule-like computation from fact-like storage. In particular, understanding under what conditions memorization localizes in specific architectural submodules — such as attention heads versus feedforward layers — remains an intriguing question. Finally, the interplay between memorization and generalization has long been studied in cognitive science and neuroscience, see e.g. [ 40 ], often framed in terms of complementary learning systems. It is an interesting avenue for future work to explore whether the RAF framework and its analysis introduced here could have interesting implications in that context. An important direction for future work is to move beyond the dichotomy between beneficial and detrimental memorization. In realistic learning systems, memorization may play multiple roles: enabling the storage of rare but essential information, or giving rise to unwanted retention of spurious or sensitive data. Our work may inspire new frameworks for controlled settings in which such distinct forms of memorization can be disentangled and studied systematically. More broadly, many tasks intrinsically require an active interaction between memorization and generalization, rather than their mere coexistence. Exceptions may refine rules, stored facts may guide inference under ambiguity, and memory may serve as a scaffold for abstraction. Understanding these mechanisms — and the architectural or dynamical principles that regulate them — represents a promising avenue toward a richer theory of learning in both artificial [ 41 ]and biological systems [40]. 19 Acknowledgment

D.5 Implementation details 

For completeness, we summarize the numerical protocol used throughout this appendix. 

Gaussian RAF data. For the synthetic RAF experiments, we generate i.i.d. Gaussian inputs xμ ∼ N (0, Id) and labels according to the RAF mixture described in Sec. 1. Kernel ridge regression is implemented by solving the regularized linear system associated with the Gram matrix, while the hinge-loss kernel classifier is trained as a standard support vector machine. Generalization error is estimated on fresh teacher-labeled Gaussian test data, whereas memorization error is computed on the random-label subset of the training set, in direct analogy with Eqs. labels. Inputs are feature-wise standardized. For Gaussian-like kernels, bandwidths are reported using the effective normalization described in Sec. 3. All empirical CIFAR trade-off curves shown here are averaged over repeated draws of the training/test split and of the randomized labels.
