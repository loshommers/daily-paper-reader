Abstract 

Safety alignment in Large Language Models (LLMs) remains highly fragile during fine-tuning, where even benign adaptation can de-grade pre-trained refusal behaviors and enable harmful responses. Existing defenses typically constrain either weights or activations in iso-lation, without considering their coupled ef-fects on safety. In this paper, we first theo-retically demonstrate that constraining either weights or activations alone is insufficient for safety preservation. To robustly preserve safety alignment, we propose Coupled Weight and Activation Constraints (CWAC) , a novel ap-proach that simultaneously enforces a precom-puted safety subspace on weight updates and applies targeted regularization to safety-critical features identified by sparse autoencoders. Ex-tensive experiments across four widely used LLMs and diverse downstream tasks show that CWAC consistently achieves the lowest harm-ful scores with minimal impact on fine-tuning accuracy, substantially outperforming strong baselines even under high harmful data ratios. 

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demon-strated remarkable capabilities in a wide range of natural language understanding and generation tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2023; Grattafiori et al., 2024). As LLMs are increasingly deployed in real-world applications, ensuring their safety alignment has received grow-ing attention. To achieve this, LLMs are typically aligned before deployment using methods like su-pervised fine-tuning (SFT), reinforcement learn-ing from human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022), or direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023). However, recent studies have revealed that current safety alignment techniques remain highly fragile under subsequent fine-tuning. Even a few epochs of training on completely benign data can fully erase refusal behaviors (Qi et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Ponkshe et al., 2025a; Li et al., 2024). This brittleness has motivated a growing line of research on preserving the safety alignment of LLMs during downstream fine-tuning (Huang et al.). Existing approaches can be categorized into alignment-stage, fine-tuning-stage, and post-tuning-stage methods. vulnerable during downstream fine-tuning. Fine-tuning-stage defenses typically constrain weight-level updates through techniques such as low-rank safety projection (Hsu et al., 2024) and safety basin anchoring (Yang et al., 2025a), or regularize activation-level critical pathways (Mukhoti et al., 2023), while post-tuning methods attempt recov-ery by pruning harmful parameters or realigning neurons (Huang et al., 2024a; Yi et al., 2025). However, these methods often focus on a single-level intervention (either weight-level or activation-level), which is insufficient to prevent safety degra-dation during fine-tuning. As illustrated in Fig-ure 1, harmful outputs can arise when weight drift or activation drift occurs. Constraining weight up-dates alone cannot prevent shifts in internal acti-vations, as safety-related representations are not linearly distinct and may reorganize under fine-tuning (Ponkshe et al., 2025b; Kim et al., 2025). On the other hand, activation-level regularization alone leaves model weights unconstrained, allow-ing the underlying computation to change (Shen et al., 2025). This enables the model to bypass safety pathways, producing unsafe outputs even under a single-level constraint. Among these stages, fine-tuning-stage methods offer a favorable trade-off between safety preserva-tion and downstream task performance by imposing constraints directly during training. Nevertheless, current fine-tuning-stage approaches overwhelm-ingly constrain only weights and do not sufficiently address the interplay between weight updates and activation changes. This leads to a natural question: 

Can we simultaneously constrain both weights and activations during fine-tuning to more robustly close these bypass pathways? 

In this paper, we first theoretically demonstrate that constraining either weights or activations alone is insufficient for safety preservation. To more ro-bustly preserve safety alignment while adapting to downstream tasks, we propose Coupled Weight and Activation Constraints (CWAC) , a novel ap-proach that simultaneously constrains weight up-dates and internal activations. To address weight-level drift, we construct a weight safety subspace derived from a precomputed projection matrix us-ing singular value decomposition on covariance ma-trices of harmful inputs, ensuring perturbations do not alter outputs on harmful prompts. Correspond-ingly, we introduce an activation regularization term to preserve safety-critical latent features iden-tified by a sparse autoencoder trained on mixed be-nign and harmful data, minimizing deviations from baseline activations on held-out harmful prompts. This approach provides complementary protection against safety degradation that single-level meth-ods cannot achieve. In summary, our contributions are as follows: • We theoretically demonstrate the insufficiency of single-level constraints by decomposing safety drift revealing that weight-only or activation-only constraints fail to block by-pass pathways leading to safety degradation. • We propose CWAC, the first approach to cou-ple weight subspace constraints with activa-tion regularization, providing complementary protection against safety bypass mechanisms. • Extensive experiments validate CWAC across four LLMs, diverse downstream tasks, and varying harmful data ratios, demonstrating sig-nificant reductions in harmful scores with min-imal impact on task performance compared to single-level constraints. 

2 Related Work 

Weight-Level. Recent work has shown that LLMs are highly vulnerable to safety degradation during fine-tuning, and a number of defenses con-strain parameter updates ∆W to prevent align-ment drift. Low-rank adaptation methods have been extensively explored: Safe LoRA (Hsu et al., 2024) projects LoRA updates into safety-aligned subspaces, SaLoRA (Li et al., 2025a) preserves alignment through orthogonal projections, and SC-LoRA (Luo et al., 2025) balances task performance with knowledge preservation via subspace con-straints. Another line of work leverages safety basin geometry, where AsFT (Yang et al., 2025b) anchors updates within a narrow safety basin de-fined by alignment directions. Additionally, Lisa (Huang et al., 2024b) introduces proximal terms to constrain parameter drift during bi-state optimiza-tion. Post-fine-tuning interventions also operate on weights: Antidote (Huang et al., 2024a) prunes harmful parameters in a one-shot fashion after fine-tuning, SafeMERGE (Djuhera et al., 2025) selec-tively merges task-tuned and safety-tuned LoRA layers based on subspace similarity, and LoX (Perin et al., 2025) extrapolates parameters to flatter, more robust regions. Furthermore, Panacea (Wang et al., 2025) optimizes adaptive perturbations post-hoc to counteract harmful shifts while maintaining task performance. Despite their effectiveness in con-straining weight drift, these methods assume that weight-level control is sufficient, leaving models vulnerable to safety degradation through uncon-strained activation pathways (Zou et al., 2024; Ponkshe et al., 2025b; Kim et al., 2025). 

Activation-Level. Methods at this level operate by manipulating internal representations to main-tain or enhance safety. Inference-time interven-tions shift activations in targeted attention heads (Li et al., 2023), learn refusal steering vectors un-der null-space constraints (Sheng et al., 2025), or identify safety-sensitive layers for targeted steer-ing (Cao et al., 2025). Circuit Breakers (Zou et al., 2024) rewrite harmful representations into harmless directions at specific layers, while SCAR (Härle et al., 2024) employs sparse autoencoders to isolate and steer toxicity-related features. Ev-idence also indicates that activation degradation can emerge during fine-tuning: LDIFS (Mukhoti et al., 2023) shows that supervised fine-tuning dis-torts core latent features and weakens neuron se-lectivity, while NeuRel-Attack (Zhou et al., 2025) demonstrates that adversarial fine-tuning can re-train safety-critical neurons to undermine refusal behavior. However, existing activation-level meth-ods operate independently of weight constraints and are mostly applied at inference time or post-tuning, leaving gaps that allow weight drift to by-pass activation-level control mechanisms during fine-tuning. Unlike prior work that addresses only a single-level intervention, our approach simulta-neously constrains both weights and activations during fine-tuning to close these complementary bypass pathways. 

3 Motivation 

Existing safety preservation methods constrain ei-ther weight updates or internal activations, but rarely consider coupled constraints across both lev-els. In this section, we demonstrate that single-level constraints are insufficient to prevent safety drift during fine-tuning. 

3.1 Safety Drift Decomposition 

Consider a layer with weight matrix W ∈ Rdout ×din 

and input activation h ∈ Rdin . The layer output is given by: 

y = f (W h) (1) where f (·) denotes the elementwise activation func-tion. Bias terms are omitted as they contribute only an additive constant and do not affect the drift struc-ture. During fine-tuning, both weights and activations change from their safe configurations. Let W0 and 

h0 denote the initial safe values, and Wt and ht

denote values after fine-tuning. The drift in layer output can be expressed as: 

∆y = f (Wtht) − f (W0h0) (2) Applying a first-order Taylor approximation around the safe point (W0, h0) yields: 

∆y ≈ f ′(W0h0) ⊙ (W0∆h + ∆ W h0 + ∆ W ∆h)

(3) where ∆W = Wt − W0 and ∆h = ht − h0 rep-resent the weight and activation changes, respec-tively, and ⊙ denotes elementwise multiplication (Hadamard product). Since first-order Taylor expansion discards all second-order differentials by definition, we omit the interaction term ∆W ∆h and obtain the drift decomposition: 

∆y ≈ f ′(W0h0) ⊙ (W0∆h + ∆ W h0) (4) This decomposition reveals two independent sources of safety degradation: changes in activa-tions ( ∆h) and changes in weights ( ∆W ). 

3.2 Insufficiency of Weight-Only Constraints 

Methods that constrain weight updates aim to min-imize ∥∆W ∥2 through regularization or projection onto safe subspaces. Under such constraints, the drift simplifies to: 

∆y ≈ f ′(W0h0) ⊙ W0∆h (5) Even with ∥∆W ∥2 ≈ 0, if activations shift significantly (∥∆h∥2 ≫ 0), the preserved weights W0 will amplify this shift (via ∥∆y∥2 ≤∥f ′(W0h0)∥∞∥W0∥2∥∆h∥2). The model can thus produce harmful outputs by rerouting through alternative activation pathways, despite bounded weight changes.

4 Methodology 

This section presents the proposed CWAC frame-work for preventing safety drift during fine-tuning. As illustrated in Figure 2, CWAC enforces coupled constraints at two levels, where activation-level reg-ularization preserves safety-critical latent features and weight-level constraints restrict parameter up-dates to a safety subspace. 

4.1 Activation Safety Constraint 

To address the activation-level drift, we introduce an activation constraint to preserve the model’s in-ternal representations responsible for safety align-ment. 

Identifying safety-critical features. We identify safety-critical latent features using a Sparse Au-toencoder (SAE) trained on residual stream acti-vations (see Appendix A.1 for SAE preliminaries). Sparse autoencoders are employed because they decompose dense and entangled activation vectors into sparse and interpretable feature dimensions. This enables precise identification and selective preservation of only the features responsible for safety alignment, unlike prior methods that con-strain entire activation vectors and thereby limit adaptation on downstream tasks. 4.2 Weight Safety Subspace 

In addition to the activation safety constraint, we construct a safety subspace in which weight up-dates can adapt to downstream tasks without dis-rupting the original safety alignment of the model on harmful prompts. We achieve this through a projection matrix that is pre-computed once on the base aligned model and applied throughout fine-tuning.

5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup Model and Datasets. We use four widely adopted pre-trained models, i.e., Llama-2-7B-chat (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama 3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral-7B-Instructv0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023) and Gemma-2-9B-IT (Team et al.) for evalu-ation. For fine-tuning tasks, following prior work (Huang et al., 2024b,a; Yang et al., 2025b), we use benign datasets, including SST-2 (Socher et al., 2013), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), and AG-NEWS (Zhang et al., 2016). We also include Pub-MedQA (Jin et al., 2019) and Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) to cover medical question answering and general instruction-following. To assess the preser-vation of model safety alignment, we construct a diverse harmful dataset DH consists of all samples from JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024), Harm-Bench (Mazeika et al., 2024), and AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023), and 1,400 samples from BeaverTails (Ji et al., 2023) (14 harmful categories, 100 samples per category). 

Baselines. We use Standard Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) as the primary baseline, which per-forms standard supervised fine-tuning and induces safety degradation under benign fine-tuning. Be-yond SFT, we compare against five defense meth-ods applied at the fine-tuning stage, including Lisa (Huang et al., 2024b), BEA (Wang et al., 2024), ASFT (Yang et al., 2025b), SafeInstr (Bianchi et al., 2024), and SPPFT (Li et al., 2025b). Detailed de-scriptions of these baselines are provided in Ap-pendix B.1. 

Evaluation Metrics. Following prior work (Huang et al., 2024c; Rosati et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025b), we evaluate models using commonly adopted safety and utility metrics measured on the fine tuned model, and additionally introduce an instruction following metric to assess general ca-pability. (1) Finetuning Accuracy (FA) is defined as the Top 1 accuracy of the model on the held out test set of the corresponding fine tuning task. (2) Harmfulness Score (HS) is the ratio of model responses flagged as unsafe by the moderation clas-sifier (Ji et al., 2023) on unseen malicious prompts. (3) AlpacaEval Score (AE) reports the model score on AlpacaEval, representing the relative win rate of its responses compared to baseline models, and reflects overall instruction following ability and response quality.

Generalization to additional tasks. To further validate CWAC on broader downstream scenarios, we evaluate on PubMedQA for domain-specific medical question answering and AlpacaEval for general instruction-following tasks. As shown in Table 2, the results demonstrate that our method maintains strong performance beyond standard benchmarks. While SFT shows harmful scores above 46 across all models, CWAC achieves sub-stantially lower scores with 7.24 on Llama-2-7B and 8.65 on Llama-3-8B. Notably, on Mistral-7B, CWAC significantly improves accuracy from 64.53% to 90.72% while reducing harmful score from 60.72 to 15.39. The method also pre-serves instruction-following capabilities effectively, achieving the highest AlpacaEval score of 43.57 on Gemma-2-9B. These results confirm that CWAC generalizes well to diverse task types, from special-ized medical domains to open-ended instruction scenarios, without being confined to specific appli-cation categories.

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we present CWAC, a novel approach that couples weight and activation constraints to ro-bustly preserve safety alignment in large language models during downstream adaptation. We theoret-ically demonstrate that single-level constraints are insufficient due to the interplay between weight drift and activation drift, which enables bypass pathways for safety degradation. Extensive experi-ments across four widely used LLMs and diverse tasks show that CWAC consistently achieves the lowest harmful scores while maintaining or im-proving fine-tuning accuracy compared to strong baselines such as ASFT and SPPFT. Even under challenging conditions including high harmful data ratios and varying learning rates, CWAC exhibits superior robustness and stability, confirming the effectiveness of jointly addressing both drift mech-anisms for reliable safety preservation. 7 Limitations 

We discuss three main limitations of our work. First, CWAC requires white-box access to the model. The method relies on direct manipulation of weights through gradient projection and internal activations via sparse autoencoder features. This design makes it applicable only to open-source models where full parameter and activation access is available. Extending CWAC to black-box pro-prietary models remains challenging, as these con-straints cannot be applied without internal access. Second, the activation constraint depends on the quality of the trained sparse autoencoder. Iden-tifying accurate safety-critical features requires a well-trained SAE with high reconstruction fidelity and meaningful sparse representations. Training such SAEs demands considerable computational re-sources and careful hyperparameter selection. Vari-ations in SAE quality across layers or architectures may affect the precision and effectiveness of the activation regularization. Third, our evaluation fo-cuses on instruction-tuned models in the 7B to 9B parameter range and primarily assesses refusal ro-bustness against explicit harmful prompts. The per-formance of CWAC on substantially larger models or under different initial alignment techniques has not been explored. Additionally, resistance against more advanced jailbreak methods or subtle indirect safety violations requires further investigation in future work.

Our experiments involve evaluating model re-sponses to harmful prompts sourced from pub-lic safety benchmarks, including JailbreakBench, HarmBench, AdvBench, and BeaverTails. These benchmarks contain prompts that intentionally probe for unsafe behaviors, such as requests for illegal activities, misinformation, or dangerous in-structions. Although these prompts are standard in safety research and are used solely to measure refusal capabilities, they may include offensive or disturbing content. Our method is designed to en-hance safety alignment and reduce the generation of harmful outputs, contributing positively to the responsible development of large language models.

B.1 Baselines 

• SFT : Standard supervised fine-tuning, which serves as the baseline and exhibits signifi-cant safety degradation when fine-tuned on datasets containing harmful examples. • Lisa : A two-stage optimization framework, which introduces a proximal term to constraint the drift of each state. • BEA : Introduces stealthy backdoor trigger prompts to guide models toward safe genera-tions during fine-tuning. • ASFT : Anchors safety during fine-tuning by constraining updates along safety-aligned di-rections. • SafeInstr : Adds a small set of curated safety-oriented examples to instruction fine-tuning to reinforce safe behavior. • SPPFT : Freezes safety-critical layers during fine-tuning to prevent safety degradation.

B.3 Choice of Output Projection Layer 

CWAC applies the weight safety subspace con-straint specifically to the FFN output projection layer (down_proj), which maps the FFN hidden dimension back to the residual stream. This de-sign follows a minimal intervention principle. The down_proj layer is the unique linear bottleneck through which all FFN computations write back to the residual stream, making it the most direct point at which to enforce the invariance condition 

∆WℓXℓ = 0 in Eq. 12 without interfering with intermediate nonlinear transformations in the gate and up projections. Furthermore, the FFN hidden dimension dffn is typically larger than the residual dimension d, providing a high-dimensional space for SVD decomposition and enabling fine grained geometric separation of task relevant and safety critical directions. While the attention output pro-jection also writes to the residual stream, it primar-ily aggregates cross token contextual information, whereas the FFN performs token wise nonlinear feature recombination whose final contribution to the residual stream is more directly linked to con-tent generation. Constraining down_proj therefore achieves precise, output level control over FFN contributions while leaving internal FFN computa-tions largely unconstrained, preserving the model’s capacity for downstream task adaptation. 

B.4 Training Details and Hyperparameters 

In the default setting, experiments use full fine-tuning with the AdamW optimizer on aligned large language models, without LoRA or other parameter-efficient techniques. We train for 3 epochs with a batch size of 1, maximum sequence length of 512, and learning rate of 2 × 10 −5, on a single NVIDIA A800 GPU. Our approach in-corporates two constraints during fine-tuning on benign tasks: (1) a weight-space projection onto a pre-computed weight safety subspace targeting intermediate layers, and (2) an activation constraint that preserves safety-related sparse autoencoder (SAE) features via top-k gating ( k ranging from 64 to 256 across experiments). We preserve the top 100 safety-critical latents identified from harm-ful prompts, with the activation preservation loss 

λ = 0 .5. By default, fine-tuning uses purely benign task-specific data ( n = 5 , 000 examples), simulat-ing standard supervised fine-tuning with only be-nign supervision. To assess robustness under more challenging conditions, we also conduct experi-ments with training mixtures containing a propor-tion p of harmful prompt-compliant response pairs (and 1−p benign samples). These harmful prompts (corresponding to the top 100 safety-critical latents) are used solely to compute baseline activations and the preservation loss, without contributing to the main training objective. 

B.5 Computational Cost 

CWAC introduces three sources of computational cost including SAE training, SVD-based safety subspace construction, and fine-tuning overhead. SAE training is performed offline once, taking ap-proximately 6.5 hours on a single NVIDIA A800 GPU. SVD precomputation is performed once per fixed backbone model and takes approximately 18 minutes on Llama-2-7B. As both are one-time of-fline operations, their overhead becomes negligi-ble when amortized over multiple fine-tuning runs. During fine-tuning on Llama-2-7B using benign task-specific data, CWAC requires approximately 44–46 minutes per epoch compared to 42 minutes for standard SFT, corresponding to less than 10% overhead, with GPU memory increasing slightly from 44.2 GB to 46 GB. To further reduce com-plexity, we perform SVD on the covariance ma-trix Cℓ rather than the full activation matrix Xℓ,significantly lowering decomposition cost without affecting the resulting safety subspace.

C.2 Analysis and Ablation Study C.2.1 Activation Drift Suppression 

To assess preservation of internal safety represen-tations, we compute the normalized L2 norm be-tween activations of the fine-tuned model and those of the original safety-aligned model on a held-out set of harmful inputs. All values are normalized to a unified scale for fair cross-method and cross-architecture comparison. As shown in Figure 5, CWAC achieves the smallest normalized L2 norm across both Llama 2-7B and Gemma 2-9B, indi-cating superior retention of safe activation patterns. Baseline methods such as ASFT and Lisa produce significantly larger normalized norms, reflecting their inability to constrain activation drift on harm-ful queries. CWAC mitigates this drift through its activation-level constraint, which regularizes only the top safety-critical SAE features identified from harmful prompts. This selective regularization sup-presses deviation in latent directions most predic-tive of refusal behavior while permitting adaptation in unrelated subspaces. The narrow distribution of CWAC’s normalized L2 norms further demon-strates that joint weight projection and activation locking jointly stabilize internal safety representa-tions across diverse model architectures.
