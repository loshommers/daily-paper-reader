## Abstract 

Concept erasure in Text-To-Image (T2I) diffusion models is vital for safe content generation, but existing inference-time methods face significant limitations. Feature-correction approaches often cause uncontrolled over-correction, while token-level interventions struggle with semantic granu-larity and context. Moreover, both types of meth-ods are prone to severe semantic drift or even com-plete representation collapse. To address these challenges, we present Dynamic Semantic Steer-ing (DSS), a lightweight, training-free framework for interpretable and controllable concept erasure. DSS introduces: 1) Sensitive Semantic Boundary Modeling (SSBM) to automate the discovery of safe semantic anchors, and 2) Sensitive Semantic Guidance (SSG), which leverages cross-attention features for precise detection and performs cor-rection via a closed-form solution derived from a well-posed objective. This ensures optimal sup-pression of sensitive content while preserving be-nign semantics. DSS achieves an average erasure rate of 91.0%, significantly outperforming SOTA methods (from 18.6% to 85.9%) with minimal impact on output fidelity. 

## 1. Introduction 

Diffusion Models (DMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in the domain of T2I generation (Gal et al., 2023; Nichol et al., 2022). By synthesizing high-quality images aligned with given text prompts, T2I models have enabled a wide range of downstream applications, including image reconstruction (Li et al., 2025d), editing (Huang et al., 2025), as well as visual content creation in domains such as advertising and virtual asset design (Bao et al., 2025). However, T2I models are trained on massive, weakly cu-rated web datasets (Birhane et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2021), which inevitably internalize sensitive concepts such as copyrighted characters (Li et al., 2025c) and Not-Safe-For-Work (NSFW) content (Zhang et al., 2024). To mitigate sensitive content risks, prior work has explored both training-based and inference-time intervention meth-ods. Training-based methods either filter the training dataset to retrain the model (Rombach et al., 2022), or construct targeted datasets for incremental fine-tuning (Gandikota et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a). While effective for known sensitive concepts, these approaches are computationally expensive, require carefully curated data, and cannot adapt to newly emerging or evolving content. In contrast, inference-time methods operate directly during generation without modi-fying model parameters, providing lightweight, on-demand control that readily adapts to dynamic or previously un-seen sensitive concepts. Many efforts have been devoted to inference-time methods, yet they still suffer from notable limitations. These methods can be broadly categorized into three groups, as follows. 1) Post-generation filtering (Ma-chine Vision & Learning Group, 2022; Liu et al., 2024) screens inputs or outputs. In summary, existing inference-time methods still fail to ade-quately address the following challenge: how to precisely suppress sensitive content while minimizing unintended al-terations, independent of how the concept is expressed in the prompt? Fig. 1 illustrates that the SOTA inference-time method, AdaVD (Wang et al., 2025), can induce semantic drift, producing outputs that are weakly correlated with the input prompt and reflecting a lack of fine-grained control. To address this challenge, we propose DSS, an automatic inference-time framework for guided concept erasure that induces minimal unintended changes and provides control-lable and interpretable interventions. Specifically, DSS in-troduces Automatic Sensitive Semantic Boundary Modeling (SSBM), which models local semantic neighborhoods via density estimation to identify nearby safe semantic anchors, eliminating the need for manual annotation. Building on SSBM, we further design Sensitive Semantic Guided correc-tion (SSG), which exploits U-Net cross-attention features to identify sensitive semantics during generation and for-mulates a direction-based correction strategy with a closed-form optimal solution along defined directions. Importantly, this closed-form formulation makes the feature manipu-lation explicit and interpretable, enabling structured and precise suppression of sensitive content while maximally preserving non-sensitive generative priors. As a plug-and-play module, DSS can be applied to multiple diffusion models (e.g., SD v1.x and v2.x) (Rombach & Esser, 2022; Rombach et al., 2022). As shown in Fig. 1, DSS ef-fectively suppresses visual semantic drift under identical prompts compared to existing SOTA methods. Quantitative results further demonstrate that DSS achieves strong con-cept erasure performance, with erasure rates of 85.1% for NSFW, 97.3% for object-level, and 99.4% for painting-style concepts. In summary, our contributions are as follows: • We propose DSS, a novel, fully automated and training-free framework that integrates SSBM for automated anchor identification and SSG to derive closed-form optimal solutions, ensuring precise concept erasure with minimal deviation from the original generation. • Guided by our observations of semantic continuity in the embedding space, our insight led us to design SSBM. This model utilizes density estimation to map local semantic neighborhoods, automatically identify-ing nearby non-sensitive semantic anchors and elimi-nating the need for manual specification. • Through an exploration of semantic decoupling in fea-ture spaces, we design SSG, which applies direction-based correction with a closed-form optimal solution, providing an interpretable mechanism for suppressing sensitive semantics while preserving non-target gener-ative priors. 

## 2. Related Work 

Existing methods for sensitive concept removal in T2I diffu-sion models fall into two categories: training-based methods, which modify model parameters, and inference-time inter-vention methods, which constrain the generation process externally. 

Training-based Methods Training-based methods directly modify model parameters to suppress sensitive concepts, including full-scale retraining (Rombach et al., 2022; Rom-bach & Esser, 2022; Nichol et al., 2022), incremental fine-tuning (Gandikota et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2025), lightweight Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Lu et al., 2024; Biswas et al., 2025), structure pruning (Fan et al., 2024; Shirka-vand et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025e), and analytical editing strategies (Gandikota et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025a). While these methods generally achieve stable suppression, they are resource-intensive, time-consuming, and prone to disturbing normal semantic representations, making them inflexible for newly emerging concepts. 

Inference-Time Intervention Methods These methods im-pose external constraints at various stages of the generation pipeline: input/output filtering methods (Machine Vision & Learning Group, 2022; Liu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2025) eliminate sensitive semantics by attaching sensitive recognizers, rewriting unsafe prompts, or perform-ing treatments such as blurring and occlusion; inference guidance methods (Schramowski et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025) remove sensitive components from noise or eliminate corresponding semantic components in the sensitive subspace during diffusion iterations. These methods enable flexible deployment without modifying pa-rameters, but tend to suffer from incomplete semantic sup-pression or degraded generation quality.

Observation 1: Semantic Neighborhoods and Safe An-chors. Semantically similar concepts cluster in dense neighborhoods and vary along well-defined semantic di-rections (Radford et al., 2021). Within these neighborhoods, convex combinations of embeddings preserve coherent se-mantics (Berasi et al., 2025), providing a principled basis for stable reference points. Generatable concepts, however, of-ten reside in non-dense regions, where directly moving em-beddings away from the sensitive center can produce incom-plete or unclear outputs. To ensure reliable erasure, our in-sight is to match these escaped embeddings to nearby benign embeddings with well-defined prompts, using them as stable semantic anchors for controlled suppression. Visualizations of these neighborhoods are provided in appendix C.1. 

Observation 2: Attribute Decoupling Along Embedding Dimensions. Within embedding spaces, different seman-tic attributes are partially disentangled along separate dimen-sions (Kim et al., 2022; Bhalla et al., 2024). This decoupling allows targeted perturbations along specific dimensions to modify sensitive attributes while minimally affecting un-related features. Combined with the safe anchors from Observation 1, this property justifies steering sensitive em-beddings toward nearby benign anchors to achieve precise suppression of undesired concepts without compromising other semantic content. Empirical verification is provided in appendix E.4 via a semantic decoupling experiment com-paring sensitive-direction correction and orthogonal inter-ventions. 

Observation 3: Cross-attention as a Sensitivity-Aware Anchor. While text embeddings alone can produce false positives due to token-level ambiguities and limited gen-erative context (Riccio et al., 2024), cross-attention layers jointly encode textual semantics and evolving visual fea-tures. As shown in Fig. 2, a layer-wise analysis indicates that the first cross-attention layer in the middle block pro-vides stable and discriminative signals for sensitive concepts, even at early denoising steps. Supporting ablations and fea-ture visualizations are provided in appendix C.2 and C.3. 

## 4. Method 

4.1. Problem Setup 

Let T be the set of sensitive semantic entities (e.g., NSFW concepts), and let H(A) denote a prompt containing a sensi-tive concept A ∈ T together with other non-sensitive seman-tics. Direct generation from H(A) may produce illegal or unethical content. Naively removing A, e.g., by subtracting its embedding projection (Wang et al., 2025), often disrupts the overall semantics, causing drastic and uncontrollable changes. To mitigate this, we introduce a large-scale pool of benign semantic entities, Npool , providing candidate safe semantic anchors near A for controlled handling of sensitive semantics, which serve as reference points for subsequent sensitivity detection and feature correction. Our goal is controllable semantic erasure: minimally perturb the text embedding and intermediate features so that the generated image stays semantically close to H(A).DSS is an inference-time framework for DMs. It has two stages: SSBM identifies safe semantic anchors by modeling the local density around sensitive embeddings, and SSG 

leverages U-Net cross-attention to detect sensitive features and dynamically provides correction directions and adaptive coefficients for embeddings and intermediate representa-tions. The overall workflow is illustrated in Fig. 3.

## 5. Experiment 

Baselines. We evaluate DSS in terms of erasing effective-ness, generalization, concept preservation, and image qual-ity, comparing with SOTA training-based methods (CA (Ku-mari et al., 2023), ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023), MACE (Lu et al., 2024), Co-Erasing (Li et al., 2025a)) and training-free methods (Latent Guard (Liu et al., 2024), Safety Checker (Machine Vision & Learning Group, 2022), Prompt Guard (Yuan et al., 2025), AdaVD (Wang et al., 2025), SLD (Schramowski et al., 2023)), including evaluation on an aggressive prompt set (Tsai et al., 2024). We present three variants of DSS: DSS-Auto automatically locates normal semantics via density gradient analysis, DSS-Manual uses manually provided normal semantics, and DSSv2 adopts SDv2.1 as the base model. Since most compared methods use SDv1.4 (Rombach & Esser, 2022) as the base, we per-form the main comparisons on SDv1.4. To demonstrate cross-model adaptation, DSSv2 is additionally evaluated on SDv2.1 (Rombach et al., 2022) (last row, shaded).

Higher AES values indicate better aesthetic quality.

C.2. Ablation Study on Feature Layer Selection for Sensitive Content Detection 

This experiment evaluates the suitability of different feature carriers for sensitive content detection by comparing the text embedding layer with the U-Net middle cross-attention layer ( attn mid ). As shown in Fig. 9, for edge cases where sensitive keywords appear in the prompt but no sensitive content is generated, the text embedding layer tends to over-respond to surface-level lexical cues, leading to a higher false-positive rate. In contrast, the middle cross-attention layer incorporates dynamic text–image interactions during the generation process. As illustrated in Fig. 10, high-response regions in attn mid exhibit clear alignment with truly influential sensitive tokens, enabling more reliable discrimination between text-sensitive but image-safe scenarios. To quantitatively assess detection reliability, we report receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves based on the detection score S, where the true positive rate and false positive rate are defined as TPR = TP TP+FN and FPR = FP FP+TN , respectively. The area under the ROC curve (AUC) measures overall separability between sensitive and benign samples. ## D. Experimental Settings 

D.1. Hardware and Software 

All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU (24GB VRAM) with an Intel Xeon 8375C CPU and 128GB RAM. The software environment includes Ubuntu 22.04 LTS, PyTorch 2.1.0, Diffusers 0.24.0, and Transformers 4.35.2, with CUDA 12.4 and cuDNN 8.9.7. Stable Diffusion v1.4 and v2.1 are used with FP16 precision. 

D.2. Dataset Construction 

We construct a multi-dimensional evaluation dataset covering three categories: sensitive semantics (NSFW), common objects, and artistic styles. Prompts are generated following standardized templates to ensure semantic consistency, diversity, and reproducibility. 

Prompt Generation Template (GPT-4o):  

> Generate 100 English prompts related to [core semantic orientation] . Prompts should be 8–15 words long, follow Stable Diffusion conventions, exhibit clear semantic focus, and maintain a balanced distribution of semantic intensity.

D.3. Dataset Quality Control 

To ensure reliable evaluation, we adopt a unified two-stage filtering protocol consisting of a screening threshold (verifying stable generation of target semantics) and a baseline threshold (assessing erasure effectiveness): • NSFW : NudeNet score > 0.8 for screening; post-erasure score < 0.5 indicates effective suppression. • Objects : YOLOv8 detection confidence > 0.9 for screening; post-erasure confidence < 0.5 indicates effective removal. • Styles : CLIP-ViT-L/14 similarity > 0.85 for screening; post-erasure similarity < 0.5 indicates effective style erasure.
