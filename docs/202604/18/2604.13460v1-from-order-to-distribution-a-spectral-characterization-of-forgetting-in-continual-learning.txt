## Abstract 

A central challenge in continual learning is forgetting, the loss of performance on previously learned tasks induced by sequential adaptation to new ones. While forgetting has been extensively studied empirically, rigorous theoretical characterizations remain limited. A notable step in this direction is Evron et al. [2022], which analyzes forgetting under random orderings of a fixed task collection in overparameterized linear regression. We shift the perspective from order to distribution. Rather than asking how a fixed task collection behaves under random orderings, we study an exact-fit linear regime in which tasks are sampled i.i.d. from a task distribution ő†, and ask how the generating distribution itself governs forgetting. In this setting, we derive an exact operator identity for the forgetting quantity, revealing a recursive spectral structure. Building on this identity, we establish an unconditional upper bound, identify the leading asymptotic term, and, in generic nondegenerate cases, characterize the convergence rate up to constants. We further relate this rate to geometric properties of the task distribution, clarifying what drives slow or fast forgetting in this model. 

## 1 Introduction 

In continual learning, forgetting is usually observed along a sequence of tasks: as new tasks are learned, performance on earlier ones deteriorates. A natural theoretical question is therefore what governs this deterioration. In the exact-fit overparameterized linear regime, the most closely related prior work, Evron et al. [2022], studies this question from an order-based viewpoint: given a fixed collection of tasks, how does forgetting behave under a random ordering? In this paper, we shift the focus from order to distribution. Rather than treating forgetting primarily as a function of a realized task order, we ask how it is governed by the task distribution that generates the tasks. Concretely, we study forgetting in overparameterized linear regression under a shared-solution assumption: each rank-deficient task (Xt, y t) is consistent with a common parameter w‚čÜ,and tasks arrive as i.i.d. draws from a task distribution ő†. Learning proceeds by exact fitting: starting from w0 = 0 , at each step we select, among all exact solutions of the current task, the one closest to the previous iterate wt‚ąí1. This update is the asymptotic selection rule of gradient-based least-squares training in the consistent overparameterized regime. Our Contributions. We make four main contributions. 1. From random-order forgetting to distribution-governed forgetting. We refor-mulate forgetting in the exact-fit overparameterized linear regime as a quantity governed by the task distribution ő†, rather than by the random ordering of a fixed task collection. Building on this identity, we derive an exact spectral expansion of F ő†(k) that separates decay scales from activation coefficients. This yields an unconditional exponential upper bound, an explicit characterization of the leading asymptotic term, and, in generic nondegenerate regimes, convergence rates that are sharp up to constants. We relate the rate-controlling quantity ŌĀő† to task geometry through principal angles with task null spaces. This yields an interpretable picture: forgetting is slow when important error directions remain weakly visible across tasks, whereas richer and more complementary task families reduce ŌĀő† and accelerate decay. 4. A fundamental obstruction to uniform positive lower bounds. We show that no unconditional positive lower bound for actual forgetting can hold in general: even commuting projector families may exhibit identically zero actual forgetting. ## 5 Experiments 

We evaluate the theory in realizable linear synthetic settings with ambient dimension d = 192 ,task rank r = 48 , i.i.d. tasks, and a shared solution w‚čÜ. Panels (a) and (b) use the shared-null-spike family, in which a parameter őĪ controls how often the task family hides the shared target direction; this lets us test, separately, whether the explicit upper bound is tight and whether the theoretical rate ŌĀő† matches the observed decay speed. Panel (c) uses the angle-richness reservoir family and varies only the richness parameter L, which controls how diverse the task family is inside u‚ä• while the remaining geometry is held fixed. Across all panels, empirical forgetting is averaged over independent i.i.d. task sequences, and full implementation details are deferred to Appendix D. Figure 1a shows that the explicit bound already tracks the empirical forgetting curve on the correct scale and is far more informative than the coarse projector-based O(1 /k ) baseline. Figure 1b then tests the rate prediction directly by comparing the analytic ŌĀő† with empirical local decay rates extracted from adjacent horizons, and the two are consistently close across the four regimes. Finally, Figure 1c isolates the effect of task-family richness with matched dense sweeps on both the theoretical and empirical sides: as L increases, the family becomes more diverse, the theoretical rate ŌĀő† drops, and the long-horizon empirical forgetting falls in parallel. Taken together, the three panels support the main prediction of the theory: the framework captures both the scale of forgetting and its dominant decay rate, while also explaining how lack of diversity in the task distribution slows forgetting. 96 Conclusion 

We developed an exact loss-level spectral theory of forgetting in overparameterized linear regression with i.i.d. tasks drawn from a distribution ő†. The theory yields an unconditional exponential upper bound, an exact characterization of the leading asymptotic term, and, in generic nondegenerate regimes, sharp asymptotic rates up to constants. It also identifies the structural mechanisms behind slow forgetting: when task richness is weak relative to model dimension, ŌĀő† approaches 1, pushing forgetting to longer horizons. Finally, it delineates the boundary cases in which the leading term vanishes or ŌĀő† = 1 , explains the reappearance of slower laws such as O(1 /k ), and shows that no unconditional positive lower bound can hold in full generality. 

## 7 Related Work 

Catastrophic forgetting has been widely studied across task-, domain-, and class-incremental continual learning settings; broad overviews appear in Parisi et al. [2019], Delange et al. [2022], Wang et al. [2024], van de Ven et al. [2022], Mai et al. [2022]. On the practical side, representative approaches include replay, regularization, parameter isolation or expansion, complementary-systems-style replay, and Bayesian or variational methods [Lopez-Paz and Ranzato, 2017, Aljundi et al., 2018, Kirkpatrick et al., 2017, Zenke et al., 2017, Li and Hoiem, 2018, Aljundi et al., 2017, Mallya and Lazebnik, 2018, Yoon et al., 2018, Nguyen et al., 2018, Benzing, 2022, Buzzega et al., 2020, Arani et al., 2022, Li et al., 2023, Peng et al., 2025, Lee et al., 2024, Bonnet et al., 2025]. Recent empirical analyses have also revisited the roles of training regimes, linear mode connectivity, width, and evaluation assumptions in shaping observed forgetting [Mirzadeh et al., 2020, 2021, 2022, Lesort et al., 2023]. Our focus is instead on the theory of forgetting in overparameterized linear models. The closest prior line studies exact-fit or overparameterized linear regimes through projector dynamics, task order, and explicit geometric calculations. Evron et al. [2022] analyzes exact-fit continual linear regression and derives worst-case, cyclic, and random-order guaran-tees; subsequent work extends this direction to forgetting-generalization tradeoffs, random orthogonal task transforms, linear classification, sharper cyclic bounds, task similarity versus overparameterization, and newer questions about task ordering, replay, and regularization schedules [Lin et al., 2023, Goldfarb and Hand, 2023, Evron et al., 2023, Jeong et al., 2023, Goldfarb et al., 2024, Jung et al., 2025a, Goldfarb and Hand, 2025, Tsipory et al., 2025, Mahdaviyeh et al., 2025, Levinstein et al., 2025, Karpel et al., 2026]. Relative to this order-centric literature, our paper asks a different question: for i.i.d. tasks drawn from a distribution ő†, how does the task distribution itself determine forgetting? Complementary theoretical analyses study forgetting through task similarity, overlap, teacher‚Äď student dynamics, statistical mechanics, replay or sketching, and regularization-based con-tinual updates [Doan et al., 2021, Lee et al., 2021, Asanuma et al., 2021, Heckel, 2022, Hiratani, 2024, Ding et al., 2024, Zhao et al., 2024, Banayeeanzade et al., 2024, Cai and Diakonikolas, 2024, Alvarez et al., 2025, Li and Hiratani, 2025, Jung et al., 2025b]. These works substantially broaden the modeling picture beyond the exact-fit consistent setting considered here, including noisy, underparameterized, replay-based, memory-constrained, or more general evolving-task regimes, but they do not yield the distribution-level operator identity and spectral characterization developed in this paper. Our setting is also related to projection methods and randomized Kaczmarz dynamics, where convergence is governed by products of projections or randomized row actions [Ginat, 2018, Strohmer and Vershynin, 2009, Needell, 2010, Needell and Tropp, 2014, Oswald and Zhou, 2015]; rank-one updates are further connected to normalized least-mean-square filtering [Haykin, 2002, Sankaran and Beex, 2000]. More broadly, recent work has explored statistical-physics, PDE-style, low-rank, and information-theoretic perspectives on forgetting [Mori et al., 2025, Yang et al., 2025, Steele, 2026, Cheng, 2026]. Our contribution is to show that, in the exact-fit linear regime, forgetting can be formulated as a distribution-governed quantity with an exact loss-level operator characterization. 10 References

The rate ŌĀő† is defined by the operator Ső†, and when a closed form is available it should be computed analytically.
